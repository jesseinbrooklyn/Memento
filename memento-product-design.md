# MEMENTO

### A Stoic Wellness App for Men

---

*"You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think."*
— Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 2.11

---

## The Problem

Most wellness and mindfulness apps are designed with a soft, ambient aesthetic and a tone that doesn't connect with a large segment of men. The alternative — the "men's self-improvement" space — is dominated by hustle culture, shallow motivational content, and pseudo-philosophy stripped of its original depth.

There is no serious digital tool that brings classical Stoic philosophy to daily life with the weight, beauty, and honesty it deserves.

## The Vision

**Memento** is a daily companion rooted in Stoic philosophy. It treats the user as an adult capable of confronting hard truths — mortality, duty, impermanence, honest self-assessment — and translating those truths into a life lived with greater purpose, presence, and integrity.

The name means "remember" in Latin. Every feature is a form of remembering: remembering you will die, remembering your principles, remembering what matters, remembering to pay attention.

## Design Philosophy

Memento is contemplative, not morbid. Serious, not aggressive. Classical, not trendy.

The aesthetic draws from vanitas paintings, Roman inscriptions, and Renaissance typography — a visual language that communicates gravity and permanence without trying to sell anything. Dark backgrounds, gold accents, bone-colored text, breathing animations that mirror life's rhythms.

Every interaction should feel like opening a well-worn leather journal, not launching a productivity app.

### Design Principles

- **Confrontational honesty over comfortable affirmation.** The app doesn't tell you everything is fine. It asks whether you're living like it matters.
- **Depth over breadth.** Fewer features, each one meaningful. No engagement metrics, no streaks, no gamification.
- **Contemplative pacing.** Subtle animations, generous whitespace, unhurried transitions. The app itself models the stillness it teaches.
- **Classical authenticity.** Every philosophical reference is historically grounded. No inspirational quotes ripped from context.
- **Simplicity of architecture.** Single-component screens. Minimal dependencies. Reliability over cleverness.

## Philosophical Framework

The app is structured around the three Stoic disciplines, as outlined by Epictetus and later organized by Pierre Hadot.

### I. Discipline of Desire — *What You Pursue*

> "Make the best use of what is in your power, and take the rest as it happens." — Epictetus

This discipline is about aligning your desires and aversions with reality. Wanting only what is within your control. Releasing attachment to what is not.

**Core question the app asks daily:** *Are you chasing the right things?*

**Features in this pillar:**

- Memento Mori (daily mortality reminder)
- Negative visualization / gratitude inversion
- Impermanence meditation
- Amor fati prompts

### II. Discipline of Action — *How You Show Up*

> "Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one." — Marcus Aurelius

This discipline governs how you fulfill your roles and responsibilities — as a father, partner, friend, colleague, citizen. It's about duty, justice, and service to others.

**Core question the app asks daily:** *Did you do what was required of you?*

**Features in this pillar:**

- Role-based daily intentions
- Evening review of conduct
- Weekly role assessment
- Legacy reflection (quarterly)

### III. Discipline of Assent — *How You See Clearly*

> "It's not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things." — Epictetus

This discipline is about the space between stimulus and response. How you interpret events, manage impressions, and maintain clarity under pressure.

**Core question the app asks daily:** *Are you seeing clearly?*

**Features in this pillar:**

- Premeditatio malorum (morning premeditation of adversity)
- Real-time reframing exercises
- View from above meditation
- Journaling with honest self-assessment prompts

## Daily Rhythm

The app structures the day into three natural phases, each tied to traditional Stoic practice.

### Morning — AWAKEN

The day begins with confrontation and preparation.

**Sequence:**

1. **Alarm / Wake Screen** — The memento mori reminder. Animated hourglass. Remaining days counter. The skull. "Remember that you will die."

2. **Premeditatio Malorum** — A brief guided exercise. The app presents a category of challenge (difficult people, unexpected setbacks, loss, frustration) and walks the user through mentally rehearsing their response. This is not anxiety — it is armor. Marcus Aurelius did this every morning.

3. **Daily Intention** — Drawing from the discipline of action, the user selects or writes one intention for the day, tied to a specific role. "As a father, I will be fully present at dinner tonight." "As a colleague, I will speak honestly in the review meeting."

4. **Daily Wisdom** — A quote from the rotating library, matched to the day's theme or season of the year.

### Midday — PAUSE

A minimal interruption. Almost ambient.

**Sequence:**

1. **Mindfulness Bell** — Inspired by Thich Nhat Hanh's practice. A gentle chime at a user-configured interval (or randomized).

2. **Single Line** — One sentence. A reminder to return. Examples: "Is this essential?" / "What is in your control right now?" / "How would you act if this were your last day?"

3. **Breath** — Optional: a 30-second breathing animation synced to a 4-7-8 or box-breathing pattern. No instruction, just the visual rhythm.

The midday pause should take no more than 15 seconds if the user chooses not to linger.

### Evening — REFLECT

The day closes with honest accounting.

**Sequence:**

1. **Conduct Review** — Three questions drawn from Seneca's evening practice:
   - What went well today?
   - Where did I fall short of my own standards?
   - What didn't matter as much as I thought it would?

2. **Gratitude Through Impermanence** — Instead of "list three things you're grateful for," the app presents something specific from the user's life and asks: "What would today have been like without this?" Negative visualization as a gratitude engine.

3. **Tomorrow's Edge** — One question: "What would I regret not doing tomorrow?" This bridges the evening reflection into the next morning's intention.

## Feature Detail

### Memento Mori Screen (Complete — V1)

The existing prototype. A daily reminder of mortality featuring a days-remaining counter based on life expectancy, personalized age-aware messaging, classical vanitas imagery with subtle breathing animations, and an ornate hourglass with animated sand.

The life expectancy algorithm uses a base of 78.5 years, adjusted by lifestyle factors: smoking, drinking, exercise, diet, and sleep habits.

### Wisdom Library

A curated library of philosophical quotes organized by theme rather than just author.

**Themes include:**

- Facing mortality
- Dealing with anger and frustration
- Finding meaning in work
- Being a better father / partner / friend
- Endurance and resilience
- Wealth, poverty, and enough
- Solitude and self-reliance
- Grief and loss
- Legacy and what endures

**Sources span:**

- Core Stoics: Marcus Aurelius, Seneca, Epictetus, Musonius Rufus, Cato
- Adjacent thinkers: Montaigne, Viktor Frankl, Lao Tzu, the Hagakure (Yamamoto Tsunetomo)
- Modern Stoic writers and interpreters

Each quote includes historical context — who said it, when, and what they were facing at the time. The philosophy should never feel abstract.

### Impermanence Meditation

A daily rotating focus on something beautiful and temporary. The app pairs a brief contemplation with imagery drawn from the wabi-sabi tradition — cracked pottery, autumn leaves, weathered stone, tidal erosion.

**Rotating subjects:**

- A season (this specific spring will never return)
- A stage of life (your child's current age, your own physical prime)
- A place (the city you live in now, a neighborhood changing)
- A relationship phase (early love, a friendship in its golden era)
- A capability (your eyesight, your ability to run, your parents' voices)

The point is not sadness. It is savoring.

### View from Above

A visual meditation drawn from Marcus Aurelius, who regularly practiced imagining his life from an ever-widening perspective.

The app presents a zooming-out animation: your current moment → your room → your building → your neighborhood → your city → your continent → Earth → the cosmos. At each level, a brief line contextualizes the shift.

This is designed to take approximately 60-90 seconds and serves as a perspective reset when the user feels consumed by a problem.

### Role Assessment

A weekly or biweekly structured reflection where the user evaluates how they're fulfilling their key roles.

**Setup:** The user defines their roles during onboarding (e.g., father, husband, engineer, friend, son, mentor). Each role can have a brief personal definition of what excellence looks like in that role.

**Assessment:** For each role, the user rates themselves on a simple scale and writes a brief reflection. The app tracks these over time, not to gamify but to reveal patterns — which roles consistently get neglected, which ones the user is growing in.

### Legacy Reflection

A quarterly exercise. Not goals in the productivity-app sense, but a contemplation of what endures.

**Prompts include:**

- What do you want to be remembered for?
- What are you building that outlasts you?
- What would you want said about you at your funeral?
- If you died today, what would remain unfinished that matters?
- What have you been putting off that your future self will regret?

This section ties the memento mori concept directly to forward action. Mortality awareness without legacy reflection is just anxiety. Together, they create urgency with direction.

### Journal

A freeform journaling space with optional Stoic prompts. The journal is private, local-first, and never gamified.

**Prompt categories:**

- Morning preparation ("What challenges might I face today, and how will I meet them?")
- Evening reflection ("Where did my judgments lead me astray today?")
- Emotional processing ("What am I avoiding confronting, and why?")
- Philosophical inquiry ("What would Epictetus say about my current frustration?")
- Gratitude through loss ("What would I miss most if I lost everything today?")

### Letters (Future / Social)

Inspired by Seneca's Letters to Lucilius — his most famous and accessible works. A feature where users write weekly letters to a friend, a future self, or their children.

Letters can optionally be shared anonymously with the community. No feeds, no likes, no engagement metrics. Just letters. A man writing honestly about what he's learning.

This is the only social feature, and it is intentionally slow and asynchronous.

## Technical Architecture

### Current Stack

- React (functional components with hooks)
- Tailwind CSS (responsive styling)
- localStorage (user data persistence)
- Base64-embedded assets (deployment reliability)
- Single-component architecture per screen

### Data Model

User data stored locally:

- **Profile:** birthdate, lifestyle factors (for life expectancy calculation), defined roles
- **Journal entries:** timestamped, optionally tagged by pillar/theme
- **Role assessments:** periodic ratings and reflections
- **Legacy reflections:** quarterly entries
- **Preferences:** notification timing, midday bell frequency, theme settings
- **Letters:** drafts and sent letters (if social feature is implemented)

## Content Strategy

### Tone of Voice

Direct. Honest. Never preachy. Never condescending.

The app speaks like a wise friend who has read deeply and suffered enough to have earned the right to be blunt. It does not motivate — it reminds. It does not comfort — it clarifies.

**Examples of tone:**

- ✓ "You have approximately 14,827 days remaining. What will you do with today?"
- ✗ "You've got this! Make today count! 💪"
- ✓ "Seneca lost his son, his wealth, and his freedom. He wrote this while exiled to Corsica."
- ✗ "Here's a great quote to start your day!"
- ✓ "You rated yourself a 2 out of 5 as a father this week. What happened?"
- ✗ "Don't worry, next week will be better!"

### Historical Depth

Every quote, exercise, and meditation in the app is historically sourced and contextualized. The user should feel that they are learning from real men who faced real pressure — not consuming motivational content.

Marcus Aurelius wrote the Meditations during a plague, on military campaign, while running an empire that was collapsing around him. Epictetus was born a slave and walked with a permanent limp from his master's abuse. Seneca was exiled, recalled, forced to serve a tyrant, and ultimately ordered to kill himself.

These are not abstract thinkers. Their philosophy was forged under conditions most modern men will never face. That context matters, and the app should never let the user forget it.

### Existing Apps (Competitive Landscape)

- **Headspace / Calm** — meditation-first, soft aesthetic, broad audience, subscription-driven
- **Daily Stoic** — content-first (articles, podcasts), lacks interactive tools
- **Strides / Habitica** — habit tracking with gamification, no philosophical grounding
- **Day One** — journaling-first, no built-in philosophical framework

Memento occupies a distinct position: philosophically grounded, aesthetically serious, designed for men who want depth rather than comfort, and structured around daily practice rather than passive content consumption.
